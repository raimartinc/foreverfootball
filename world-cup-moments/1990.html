World Cup 1990: West Germany’s Roman Triumph

Italia ’90 was West Germany’s third crown—a mad mix of clutch moments, tears, and proper underdog chaos that still gets us going.

1. Brehme’s Final Penalty

Right, picture this—July 8, 1990, Stadio Olimpico in Rome, 73,603 packed in tight, West Germany vs. Argentina, final on a knife-edge. It’s 0-0, extra time’s looming—everyone’s knackered—then 85th minute rolls around. Rudi Völler’s charging, gets clipped by Roberto Sensini—penalty. Up steps Andreas Brehme, 29, cool as you like—slots it low past Sergio Goycochea—1-0. Argentina’s scrambling—Diego Maradona’s limping about, knackered—West Germany hang on. Third title in the bag, first for a unified Germany just around the corner.

Rome goes mental—German flags everywhere—Brehme’s pumping his fist, Franz Beckenbauer’s grinning like—“He’s ice-cold, that lad.” Goycochea’s sprawled, gutted—“He killed us,” Maradona mutters later. Clips from that night—Brehme’s calm strike, Jürgen Klinsmann hoisting the cup, tears streaming—it’s pure drama. “For the team,” Brehme said; Lothar Matthäus is all—“12 years paid back.” West Germany bossed it—54% possession—and Argentina couldn’t touch ‘em. “He’s clutch,” Pelé reckoned—those replays still hit hard, mate—Rome’s roaring as Germany unite.

2. Schillaci’s Italian Fire

So, Italia ’90 kicks off—June 9, and this bloke Salvatore “Totò” Schillaci, 25, Juventus bench-warmer, steps into the spotlight. Starts with Austria—74th minute, pops up, heads it in—1-0—Italy’s off. Then he’s at it again—brace against Czechoslovakia—2-0—keeps going—Uruguay, Ireland, even Argentina in the semis—67th, 1-1—Italy lose 4-3 on pens, gutting. Third-place game—2-1 over England—Schillaci’s on six goals—Golden Boot’s his. Unknown to legend, just like that.

Stadiums are buzzing—Italy’s going wild—Totò’s got these crazy eyes, like he’s possessed. “A dream,” he’s grinning; Azeglio Vicini, the gaffer, is all—“He’s our spark.” You see those clips—headers, volleys—he’s everywhere. “He’s unstoppable,” Claudio Caniggia said—Argentina’s star man felt it. Italy don’t lift the cup, but Schillaci’s fire—Golden Ball runner-up—lights up ’90. “I lived it,” he said later—six goals, no title, still a bloody hero.

3. Maradona’s Penalty Tears

Back to that final—July 8, Rome—Argentina’s trailing 1-0, Brehme’s pen’s sunk ‘em, five minutes left. Diego Maradona, 29, is hobbling—fouled to bits all game—last roll of the dice. Penalties loom—Pedro Monzón’s red-carded, Gustavo Dezotti follows—down to nine men. Shootout—Goycochea saves two—Diego nails his—but Sergio Troglio and Roberto Donadoni fluff it—4-3 West Germany. Maradona’s on the pitch, bawling—title’s gone.

It’s dead quiet—Argentina’s fans slump; Germany’s going nuts—Klinsmann’s lifting the cup. “It’s over,” Diego sobs; Carlos Bilardo, his gaffer, just sighs—“He fought.” Those clips—tears streaming, pens flying—’86’s king crashes. “They targeted him,” Caniggia said; Matthäus shrugs—“Tough luck.” Five assists, no goals—Diego’s reign ends in Rome, weeping—still gets you right in the guts, mate.

4. England’s Gazza Tears

July 4, 1990—Turin, Stadio Delle Alpi, 62,628 holding their breath—England vs. West Germany, semi-final. It’s 1-1—David Platt’s header at 81st cancels Brehme’s 60th—extra time’s ticking. Paul Gascoigne, 23—Gazza—flies in on Thomas Berthold—yellow card—his second—means he’s out of the final if England make it. Pens come—Stuart Pearce, Chris Waddle miss—4-3 Germany—Gazza’s bawling, Lineker consoling him.

Turin’s hushed—England’s gutted—Gazza’s sobbing like a kid; Bobby Robson’s muttering—“He’s just a lad.” Clips hit the telly—tears, pens—it’s ’90’s rawest bit. “He’s our soul,” Lineker said; Klinsmann’s like—“Tough break.” England lose third place—2-1 to Italy—but Gazza’s tears stick. “I broke,” he said later—still gets you, that—England’s fourth, so close, yet miles off.

5. Cameroon’s Milla Magic

June 23, 1990—Naples, San Paolo, 50,026 buzzing—Cameroon vs. Colombia, round of 16. It’s 0-0—extra time—then Roger Milla, 38, comes off the bench. 106th minute—he nicks it off René Higuita, slots it—1-0—dances at the flag like a legend. Three minutes later—109th—he’s at it again—2-0. Bernardo Redín pulls one—2-1—but Cameroon hold—first African quarter-final, lose 3-2 to England next.

Naples is rocking—Cameroon’s green’s everywhere—Milla’s hip-shaking, grinning—“For Africa.” Valery Nepomnyashchy, the gaffer, is all—“He’s our king.” Clips show it—Milla’s strikes, Higuita’s howler—’90’s underdog moment. “He’s too good,” Carlos Valderrama said; four goals total—Milla’s the oldest scorer ever. “He’s a legend,” Pelé reckoned—Cameroon stun everyone, mate—pure magic.

6. Goycochea’s Penalty Heroics

July 3, 1990—Naples again, San Paolo, 55,977 buzzing—Argentina vs. Italy, semi-final. It’s 1-1—Schillaci at 17th, Caniggia at 67th—extra time’s done. Penalties—Sergio Goycochea, 26, sub for injured Nery Pumpido, steps up—saves Roberto Donadoni, then Aldo Serena—4-3 Argentina. Before that—quarters vs. Yugoslavia—two more saves—3-2—he’s dragging Argentina to the final.

The place erupts—Argentina’s fans roaring—Goycochea’s thumping his chest—“For Diego.” Bilardo’s beaming—“He’s our wall.” Clips roll—dives, saves—Italy’s dream’s dust. “He broke us,” Gianluca Vialli said; Maradona’s grinning—“My hero.” Four shootout saves—Goycochea’s hands carry ‘em—lose the final, sure, but this? Bloody clutch, mate.
